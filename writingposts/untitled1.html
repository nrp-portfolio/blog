Untitled 1


Incomplete; Last updated 6/21/26


1

He was nine years old when he died, alone in his bed. His mother crept in quietly, footsteps muffled against the wall-to-wall plush carpeting, and stuffed a steak knife again and again into his abdomen. If he were alive to wake in the morning, he would find his room the exact same: trophies and Legos and graphic novels stacked on his nightstand, his backpack waiting patiently by the door like a dog. Only his sheets would be different, stained red the same way everyone else’s have been for one reason or another. 

2

That was the year his teacher taught him how to peel an orange. How to split the soft flesh with his fingers; how to tear into it with his teeth like an apple, wetness dripping down his chin and the front of his shirt; how to hold still and let the hands slide down the fabric clinging to his chest, to take it like a man; how to keep his lips closed around the sweetness until he forgets how to breathe.
Fruits turn sugary and alcoholic when they rot. He found an uneaten apple laying in the grass outside of his school one day. A week of eighty-degree weather and direct sunlight had turned its skin dark and leathery, its insides swollen against the wrinkled folds. He stomped down on it with the heel of his sneaker, over and over, the sweet milky froth seeping into his shoe.
His mom still packed his lunches in the mornings, which meant she didn’t hate him, but some days it felt like it. Every weekday, like clockwork, he’d go all quiet at lunch. He’d pick at his nails, the scabs on his knees, the flaking paint of the railing, then slip away to climb the stairs, back to class. A thumb slipped between his lips, filling his mouth with the taste of salt and fruit, and it was almost like a kiss.

3

And soon he’ll be fifteen, his hand wrapping around his bedsheets - new ones, dark enough the splotches of red wash out - desperately as he lets out another soft prayer. This isn’t what he had imagined for himself, but it’s better than starving. Once he’s done he’ll spend the rest of the night smoking away the scent of citrus still sticky on his fingers, passing the e-cigarette back and forth with whoever it was this time. 
His friend tells him that he has pretty hands when they’re finished. Bony and thin, knuckles blooming salmon against brown. Fingers long enough to curl around a neck, a clementine, and squeeze tight until something happens. He’s fluid, his friend says - and he runs a fingertip against his skin to illustrate the point - the curve of his jaw down to his collarbone, to his navel, to the bones jutting out right above his hips. He listens quietly as the thought settles into his stomach.
Your luck has run out when they start noticing your body, he knows, even though these sorts of things don’t happen to boys.
