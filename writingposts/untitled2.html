August 11th 1948


/ I used to like the mornings, the slowness that they brought. Eight A.M., sunlight slanting through the blinds on the window across the room at an angle that slipped just under my eyelids. My headaches would dull as I sipped the cold, milky coffee left on the table by my bedside. (Not enough sugar. Remember that, remember to buy some, when it’s cheaper, when the rations allow for flexibility. We can pool our cards together.) I would rise and dress and the house would smell like a distant memory of a hand on my cheek. 
I would repeat the details, you remember the positions, under my breath, whisper the path of the trail that those fingers carved down my jaw into the back of my hand, stifle a smile; on particularly good mornings I could close my eyes and would have no need to remember. You know this story. What else do I have to tell you? Life is boring here. An adventure—what was war like, really? My letters were never addressed to me. /

Everything is grayer in Paris. I keep my curtains closed against the light—no one ever expects me these days, so there’s no reason to allow nature to regulate my sleep-wake cycle. I can read until what appears to be midnight, peek through the fabric to catch a glimpse of the stars before going to sleep, and find only one staring down at me from the middle of the sky. On the days where I wake with the sun, I fix myself a cup of black coffee, scalding hot, and puff smoke like a Frenchman across the empty rooms of my apartment; I keep the curtains drawn no matter what. 
I spend my time writing letters and reading paperback originals and walking, when I can bring myself to leave the apartment. I still do not speak French. On the street I can catch simple words—l’eau, gauche/droit, a missing chat—je ne sais pas, mais c’est la vie. I stumble through boulangeries as if slow and I stumble through streets as if drunk and I stumble through my doorway, drunk. The receipts for the bottles scattered in the entryway of my apartment are kept in the apartment’s unused kitchenette; every week I tally them up against the remaining funds from the life insurance policy.
My letters are always addressed to one of two people. The first, and the one I most commonly return to, is long gone. A dream. Those letters wrap around themselves in that way, half-conscious and half-forgotten, trapped in-between the moments I wake up and force open my eyelids. Repeating the same stories that have nothing to do with the person they’re addressed to. I’m searching for something, I think I have to be, but every day that passes I forget a detail and fill in the sculpture’s cracks with new clay, and so every word overwrites its predecessor and every paragraph is more a lie. It is what it is. 
The second is my mother. I tell her of my days in detail, the people that I do not speak to, the gray Parisian skies and Parisian walls, too, once you pick away their paint. Every time my complaints bore her, she diagnoses me, half-ironically, with neurasthenia. Her advice is to find an English girl. Find a French girl who is fluent enough to bear. Find a French girl who does not mind a mute husband, or one who speaks in tongues. And what I mean to reply is, I would if I was capable, but what I do say is, I will when I am. 
The isolation is not completely self-imposed. This city is filled with collective mourning, stories of executions and deaths fill the weekly paper, but I’m an American, and we don’t dole out our grief in the same way. I could invent a woman, killed in childbirth, a version of myself who knew how to love, who watched the tears dry underneath the bags of her eyes, watched the blush on my newborn son’s cheeks fade, and still, what would be the point? Everyone falls asleep and wakes up, and I would remain free to remarry. Everyone has their affairs, never stumbles and trips over one woman’s thighs. It would be suspicious to mourn without an end in sight, is what I’m getting at.

My apartment has changed tonight. A spiderweb crack in the floorboards. An arachnid scuttles across the wood, making her way around and around, thread snagging and tangling to catch her prey. The palm tilted out towards the ceiling, waiting for communion. Its digits are splayed out one by one, counting up the seconds until I take another breath. Sticking out from under my bed like a taunt, a children’s fairytale monster. My fingers press into the cold fat of the visible wrist, searching for a pulse I know I won’t find. I don’t pale or flinch, my stomach doesn’t drop. We’re all so medicated now, from the newspaper prints and casualty reports just doing their jobs, that I don’t think very many people would. What do I know about war, though? I suppose I haven’t seen very many corpses in person. 
I wrap my own fingers around the arm, and tug. It twists in its socket, angled unnaturally against its bare torso. I grasp at its stomach, its hips, its ribs, pull. The head knocks against one of the bed’s legs. It rolls back and forth as I work, gyroscope broken. Finally it’s free, and I stand up. Allow myself a full look.
There—a face I don’t see often, unless shaving. And that’s odd, because he’s clean-shaven, and I’m not, and that’s odd too, because there shouldn’t be any correlation at all. 

August 15th 1948


/ You know I’m no good at letters. How do I write a flashback? How do I tell a story that you already know and that will never be read? Perhaps I will send them, addressed to your name, no-longer-your address. Confuse a newlywed housewife, or whichever distant relative inherited the deed. Your parents moved out—I suppose you couldn’t have known that. I assume they wouldn’t have been able to bear it. I wouldn’t have either, but maybe your childhood bedroom smelled of you, and maybe that would have coerced me to remain in Nebraska. 
I’m forgetting more and more of you. Will revisiting those nights iron them out, or run the paint together—chalk on a hill in the rain, somehow both paste and watercolor. A suitably American-suburb metaphor for you, I hope. I’m already wasting ink, and letting the memory pool on this page surely couldn't hurt more than letting it fade passively under the sun. 
I remember the look on your face at that dance. Neither of us knew what we were back then. And—
Now this is where the challenge lies. Do I lie? Or omit what made it what it became? Omission wouldn’t be false here. It wasn’t the thrill, surely, that sobered me. If you were anything different, but retained that quality of you, I suppose you could’ve been anyone. A freckled blonde with ringlets and a southern drawl. A similarly dark-skinned brunette, your tight curls, but a less cruel smile. You could have been rude or sickly sweet, too-much-sucre café au lait sweet,
